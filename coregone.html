Lake Baikal Whitefish

Additional Details

Lake Baikal Whitefish (Coregonus baicalensis) is a species of freshwater fish endemic to Lake Baikal, located in Siberia, Russia. It belongs to the Salmonidae family and is known for its adaptation to a unique environment and ecological significance.

Appearance and Size: The Lake Baikal Whitefish has a fusiform body, slightly compressed laterally, and a silvery coloration. Its size varies, but adult individuals typically measure between 20 to 35 centimeters in length.

Geographic Distribution: This species is strictly endemic to Lake Baikal, which is one of the world's oldest and deepest lakes, located in eastern Siberia, Russia.

Diet: Lake Baikal Whitefish primarily feed on zooplankton, small crustaceans, and aquatic insects. Their filter-feeding jaws are adapted to capture these prey in the clear waters of Lake Baikal.

Behavior: Lake Baikal Whitefish live in large schools in the deep, cold waters of Lake Baikal. They are known for their seasonal vertical migrations, moving to deeper waters in winter and returning to the surface in summer.

Reproduction: Reproduction of Lake Baikal Whitefish typically occurs in winter when the fish migrate to specific spawning areas in Lake Baikal. Eggs are deposited in the lake's substrate, where they hatch into larvae.

Conservation: Lake Baikal Whitefish is considered a species of concern due to habitat loss, pollution, and commercial fishing pressures. Conservation efforts are underway to protect this species and its unique environment.

Ecological Significance: Lake Baikal Whitefish play a key role in the Lake Baikal ecosystem as predators of zooplankton, helping maintain the balance of planktonic organisms.
